Additional Information

Does the trendline adequately describe the data (R2 value)

To check how accurately the equation and trendline you found correspond to the actual data you found, determining the R2 value is helpful.  The R2 value is the proportion of variance between the dependent and independent variables, or more plainly, how accurately does the equation reflect the data.

  1. To determine the R2 value, double click on the trendline in your plot.

Φ Notice: A menu should appear on the right

  1. Select the tab that looks like a bar graph
  2. Under the ‘Trendline Options’ list, at the bottom below where you checked to display the equation is another box to display an ‘R2 value on chart’ select this.

  1. If you now look on your chart next to where your equation is displayed you will now see something that says R2 = 0.477, or something similar.

What does the equation mean in relation to the data in real life. i.e (y increases in respect to x or etc.)

  1. When you are looking at your R2 value the closer your equation matches the data the closer R2 is to 1.

Φ Notice: Some sets of data are better portrayed using other trendline options such as polynomials, or exponential curves.  However regardless of what trendline you use sometime your R2 value wont substantially close with 1, in this case do the best you can and make do with the equation and trendline you generated, as sometimes more data is required in order to improve an R2 value

  1. If we look at the equation there are several things we can immediately attempt to infer from it, namely the maximum temperature for a day when the minimum temperature is 0 °F would be 33.629 °F.

Φ Notice: This is the same as the point where the equation crosses the y-axis on our graph

  1. The second is we can immediately assume that if the minimum temperature of the day increases, the maximum does as well.

Φ Notice: This is due to the equations x coefficient being greater than 0
